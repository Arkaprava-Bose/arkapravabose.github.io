## Motivation

In studies of synchronization and collective dynamics it is often useful to compare not only the phases of oscillations but also in their distribution of power across frequencies. Two oscillators may have same dominant frequency but differeing substantially in their subsequent harmonic content or broadband spectra. 
To quantify such differences, we employ Jensen  Shannon Divergence(JSD) gives a information theoretic measure of similarity between two probability distributions. We imitate a probability distribution by normalizing the PSD. 
While doing so we must keep in mind to remove the DC part of the spectra and analyse the oscillating part only.

---
## Defination
Given two pdfs $P$ and $Q$, we define their average distribution

$$
M = \frac{1}{2} (P+Q)
$$

The Jensen-Shannon Divergence is 

$$
\mathrm{JSD}(P,Q) = \frac{1}{2}D_{KL}(P\|M) + \frac{1}{2}D_{KL}(Q\|M)
$$

where

$$
D_{KL}(P\|Q) = \sum_i P_i \log_2\left(\frac{P_i}{Q_i}\right) 
$$

is the Kullback-Leibler Divergence.

Equivalently,

$$
\mathrm{JSD}(P,Q) = H(M) - \frac{1}{2}H(P) - \frac{1}{2}H(Q)
$$

where

$$
H(P) = -\sum_i P_i \log_2(P_i)
$$

is the shannon entropy of P

$$
\sum_{k=1}^\infty |\langle x, e_k \rangle|^2 \leq \|x\|^2
$$

Using $\log_2$ measures the JSD in bits and conviniently normalizes it to the range $0 <= JSD <=1$ where 0 determines identical distributions and 1 denotes maximally distinct distributions.

---
## Properties
The Jensen–Shannon divergence possesses several desirable properties:

- Symmetric:\\
$$
\mathrm{JSD}(P,Q)
=
\mathrm{JSD}(Q,P)
$$

- Non-negative:\\
$$
\mathrm{JSD}(P,Q)\ge0
$$

- Bounded:\\
$$
0 \le \mathrm{JSD}(P,Q)\le1
$$

when logarithms are taken in base 2.
- Finite even when some probabilities vanish.
The square root

$$
d_{JS}(P,Q)
=
\sqrt{\mathrm{JSD}(P,Q)}
$$

defines a true metric on the space of probability distributions.

---
## Application to Power Spectra Analysis
For each node of the network, PSD is computed using welch's method. Since JSD expects probability distribution, we normalize the PSD by

$$
P(f) = \frac {S(f)}{\sum_f S(f)}
$$

where $S(f)$ denotes the PSD. Prior to normalization, the mean of the PSD is removed to eliminate DC component. 
Consequently, the resulting distributions describe the allocation of power among the oscillatory frequencies. 
The pairwise Jensen–Shannon divergence between normalized spectra $P_i(f)$ and $P_j(f)$ provides a measure of spectral similarity between nodes.

---
## Use in the Present Work
For each pair of nodes, the Jensen–Shannon divergence between normalized PSDs is computed, yielding a pairwise divergence matrix.

The mean off-diagonal value

$$
\langle \mathrm{JSD} \rangle = \frac{1}{N(N-1)} \sum_{i\neq j}\mathrm{JSD}_{ij}
$$
is used as a scalar measure of spectral diversity across the network.
- Small values indicate that nodes possess similar spectral content.
- Large values indicate heterogeneous dynamical behavior and possible coexistence of different oscillatory regimes.

Thus, the JSD complements phase-based synchronization measures by characterizing similarity in frequency content rather than phase locking.
